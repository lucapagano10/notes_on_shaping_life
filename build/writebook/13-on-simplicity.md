# On Simplicity

When in doubt, subtract. Perfection is not when there's nothing left to add. It's when there's nothing left to remove. Most complexity is unnecessary. We manage it instead of eliminating it because deletion takes a kind of courage that addition doesn't. Adding feels like progress. Removing feels like loss. But removing is usually the right move. Complex things collapse under their own weight. Companies with processes so complicated they end up forgotten. Habit trackers and routines so elaborate they're easy to forget and impossible to maintain. Things that aren't simple invite excuses. The key to simplicity is that starting should feel obvious, maintaining should be the default, and building should happen step by step. Momentum after that is your best friend. What would this look like if it were easy? We have this instinct to complicate things. To add steps. To make it harder than it needs to be. But usually the elegant solution is simpler, not more complex. Make every detail perfect. And limit the number of details. You can't perfect a hundred things. You can perfect a few. Complexity is often just cowardice. It hedges. It covers every base. It avoids the hard decision about what actually matters. Simplicity forces clarity. This applies everywhere. Your schedule. Your commitments. Your goals. Your systems. Simplify until it hurts. Then simplify more.
