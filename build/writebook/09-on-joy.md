# On Joy

Become less suspicious of joy. We distort joy just as much as we distort pain. Something good happens. Watch what your mind does: it looks for the catch. It whispers that this can't last, that you don't deserve it, that something bad is coming to balance it out. We're suspicious of our own happiness. Isn't that strange? You refuse to enjoy what's good because you're already bracing for its end. You dilute the joy with anticipated loss. The moment of joy and the moment of loss are different moments. You don't have to pre-suffer. You don't have to hedge your happiness. When something good comes, let it land. Really land. Don't immediately move to the next thing. Don't qualify it. Don't explain why it doesn't really count. Just receive it. Notice how hard that is. The mind wants to minimize. Protect. Move on. I think about it like surfing. You catch a wave and you know it's going to end. That's not a reason to not ride it. You ride it fully, and you trust that more waves are coming. Happiness equals your current state minus your expectations. Which means there are two ways to be happier: improve your state, or drop your expectations. Most people only work on the first one, when the second is free.

final- notes on shaping life                                                                            4

When the goal is to experience, everything is a win. The dinner that didn't go as planned. The trip where it rained. The project that flopped but taught you something. When you focus on collecting experiences instead of outcomes, the math changes completely. We work so hard for so long for such brief moments of triumph. And then we barely let ourselves enjoy them before moving on to the next thing we need to achieve. Comparison kills joy too. The fastest way to ruin something good is to measure it against what someone else has. Your win stops feeling like a win the second you notice someone else's is bigger. Comparison is not information. It’s poison. So please, lets all become less suspicious of joy.
