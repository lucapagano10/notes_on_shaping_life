# On Winning

Here's a question that kind of broke my brain when I first heard it. How do I win even if I lose?

Most people design their lives for success. They optimize for the best case. And when things blow up, as things tend to do, they're just ruined. Standing in the rubble going, but I did everything right. The rare ones do something different. They design for non-ruin. They set it up so that even when something fails spectacularly, publicly, embarrassingly, they somehow still come out ahead. Before I start anything that matters, I ask myself three things. First: does this expand or shrink my options? Good decisions open doors. Bad ones close them. Say you try to start a company and it doesn't work. But did you meet people? Learn things? Build credibility? Get better at stuff? Then you're better positioned than before. The failure expanded your options. But say you take something that burns bridges, locks you into one path, makes you dependent on a single outcome. Even if it succeeds by normal measures, you're more fragile now. Your options shrank. That's the dangerous one. I try to choose option-expanding paths over outcome-dependent ones. Even when the outcome-dependent one looks shinier. Second: what do I still own if this goes to zero? This is the question that changed everything for me. Strip away the fantasy. Assume the startup fails. The product flops. The thing you're excited about goes nowhere. What remains? If the answer is a bigger network, new skills, trust, credibility, experience, distribution, you're safe. You literally cannot lose. The worst case still leaves you ahead of where you started. If the answer is nothing, or worse, debt and burned bridges and shame, maybe redesign this thing before jumping in. You're not evaluating whether something will succeed. You're evaluating what you keep when it doesn't. Third: am I playing a short game or a long game? Short games are money, titles, status, one-off wins. They feel good now. Long games are reputation, relationships, trust, position. They pay forever.

The best players I've watched sacrifice short games constantly. They look stupid in the moment. They look like geniuses across the decade. Everything you do pays you in different currencies. Most people only see money. But there's also network, audience, skill, trust, access, leverage. When something fails but you gained relationships, learning, and credibility, you got paid. Just not in cash. Projects that pay in only one currency are fragile. Projects that pay in many are antifragile. You want the second kind. Now here's the trap. People lose because they let outcomes define who they are. "If this fails, I failed." That sentence turns every decision into an existential threat. And when everything is existential, you play scared. You don't take the swings you should take. The antidote is to see yourself as a person who runs experiments. Experiments don't fail. They give data. Data makes you sharper. Sharp people compound. Wealth compounds. But position compounds faster. Position means people know you. Trust you. Deals flow to you. You have reach. Once you have position, almost any idea can work. Without it, even great ideas struggle. So every serious project should build position, not just profit. Profit is a symptom. Position is the cause. Here's my final test before committing to anything big. If this fails publicly and painfully, will I still be ahead two years from now? If yes, do it. Do it boldly. If no, redesign it. Make it cheaper. Add learning. Add relationships. Add optionality. Keep redesigning until it becomes a bet you can't regret. The real secret? Winning is kind of overrated. The real superpower is not being able to lose. When you can't lose, you play bigger. When you play bigger, you see more. When you see more, better opportunities find you. And one day, almost accidentally, you realize you won.
