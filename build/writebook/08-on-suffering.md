# On Suffering

We suffer more in imagination than in reality. Think about your past worries. The stuff that kept you up at night. The disasters you rehearsed over and over in your head. How many of them actually happened? And the ones that did happen. Were they as bad as you imagined? And yet here you are. Worrying again. The mind just doesn't learn this lesson on its own. You have to keep teaching it. Something I notice a lot: when I think about past problems, they never seem that big. Doesn't matter if they were or not at the time. Seen from here, they shrunk. And the good things that happened always feel huge. The mind distorts in both directions, but imaginary suffering always takes up more space than it deserves. The way I see it, life shoots the first arrow. Something hard happens. Okay. That's real. But then you shoot the second arrow into yourself. The story about what it means. The fear of what comes next. The judgment of yourself for even struggling with it. The second arrow does more damage than the first. And you're the one holding the bow. Anxiety is basically time travel. It's the gap between now and some imagined future, filled with problems that don't exist yet. And might never exist. Right here, right now, most of what's torturing you isn't real. So next time you're suffering, ask yourself: is this actually happening? Or am I rehearsing? If it's rehearsal, stop. The rehearsal doesn't prepare you. Doesn't make you stronger. It just makes you suffer twice: once in your head, once for real. And sometimes only in your head, because the real thing never shows up. When the bad thing comes, if it comes, you'll handle it. You always have.

But there's another kind of suffering. The useful kind that comes from doing something that matters. Bravery feels like fear. The difference is what you do with it. We suffer believing that fear is a signal that something's wrong. But sometimes fear is the signal that you're exactly where you need to be. Pressure is a privilege. If you're feeling the weight of something, it's because it matters. The person with nothing at stake has no anxiety, but they also have no game. The suffering that comes from growth is different from the suffering you invent at 3am. One is the price of admission. The other is a ticket to a show that doesn't exist. Learn to tell them apart. Refuse one. Welcome the other.
