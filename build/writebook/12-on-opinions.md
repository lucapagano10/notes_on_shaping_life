# On Opinions

Be curious, not judgmental. You always own the option of having no opinion. Not every situation needs your verdict. Not every controversy requires you to pick a side. You can just observe. Most of what you have opinions about doesn't actually benefit from your opinion. And every opinion costs attention. Most of them earn nothing back. The biggest obstacle to learning is thinking you already know. Because when you think you already understand something, you stop looking. You defend instead of discover. You judge instead of observe. Mark Twain says it much better: “It ain't what you don't know that gets you into trouble. It's what you know for sure that just ain't so.“ Curiosity and judgment can't coexist. When you're genuinely curious about something, like why does this person think this way, what am I missing, you're not judging. You're investigating. Investigation is open. Judgment is closed. To be interesting, be interested. Next time you feel the pull to judge, just ask: do I actually need an opinion here? Often the answer is no. And in that no is a strange kind of relief.
