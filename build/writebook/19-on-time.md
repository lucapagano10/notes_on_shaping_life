# On Time

The most rewarding things in life take years. Only bad things happen quickly. Learning something new. Changing old behaviors. Building real relationships. Mastering a craft. All of it takes time. Usually a long time. Patience isn't a nice quality. It's a primary virtue. Without it, nothing meaningful gets built. There's a concept I think about constantly. Low time preference. It means choosing tomorrow over today. Delayed gratification. Not as a single hard decision, but as an orientation to your whole life. I think it's the most powerful concept there is. Focus helps, but this is the foundation. Think about the real leverage you have in life. Almost all of it comes from long term work. This journal is a good example. If I thought about it short term, what it adds to any single day, it's worth nothing. But I look back at the end of the year and I have a full year of thoughts and notes. Low time preference plus focus is a superpower. People with low time preference play long term games with long term people. They let compounding do the work. They're not fighting against time. They're using it. Warren Buffett said something about his investing strategy that stuck with me. It's very simple. Anyone could copy it. The thing is, it requires accepting that you'll get rich slowly. And nobody wants that.

The strategy is available to everyone. The patience is available to almost no one. Success is a lagging indicator. You don't rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems. The results you see today were caused by systems you built years ago. And the systems you build today won't show up until later. This delay fools everyone. They quit before the lag catches up. They never see what would have happened if they'd stayed. We overestimate what we can do short term and underestimate what we can do long term. Here's what actually works. Show up. Do the work. Go home. A blue collar ethic married to indomitable will. Nothing interferes. Nothing sways you. Once the decision is made, you refuse to budge. No drama. No beating yourself up over small bumps. Just the work, day after day. And learn to enjoy the process. Because you're going to spend way more time on the journey than in those brief moments of triumph at the end. If you can't love the walk, you're not going to make it to the destination. Rockets use most of their fuel in the first minute of flight to escape the pull of gravity. Once they get outside that pull, the rest is effortless. Same with our habits. The beginning is where it costs the most. After that, momentum carries you. Skill compounds. Trust compounds. Knowledge compounds. But only if you stay. The edge is not in knowing what to do. It's in the patience to keep doing it. Suddenly, then all at once. That's how everything that matters arrives.
