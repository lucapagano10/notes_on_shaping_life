# On Joy


Become less suspicious of joy. We distort joy just as much as we distort pain. Something good happens. Watch what your mind does: it looks for the catch. It whispers that this can't last, that you don't deserve it, that something bad is coming to balance it out. We're suspicious of our own happiness. Isn't that strange?

You refuse to enjoy what's good because you're already bracing for its end. You dilute the joy with anticipated loss. The moment of joy and the moment of loss are different moments. You don't have to pre-suffer. You don't have to hedge your happiness.

When something good comes, let it land. Really land. Don't immediately move to the next thing. Just receive it. Notice how hard that is. The mind wants to minimize and move on.

To have more joy, you can also expect less. Drop your expectations, hug what you have, and be in the moment. 
When the goal is to experience, everything is a win. When you focus on collecting experiences instead of outcomes, the math changes completely. We work so hard for so long for such brief moments of triumph. And then we barely let ourselves enjoy them before moving on to the next thing we need to achieve. 
Comparison kills joy too. The fastest way to ruin something good is to measure it against what someone else has. Your win stops feeling like a win the second you notice someone else's is bigger. Comparison is the killer of joy.
So please, let's all become less suspicious of joy.
