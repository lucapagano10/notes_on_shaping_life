# On Suffering


We suffer more in imagination than in reality. Think about your past worries. The stuff that kept you up at night. The disasters you rehearsed over and over in your head. How many of them actually happened? And the ones that did happen. Were they as bad as you imagined? And yet here you are. Worrying again.

The mind just doesn't learn this lesson on its own. You have to keep teaching it. Something I notice a lot: when I think about past problems, they never seem that big. Doesn't matter if they were or not at the time. Seen from here, they shrunk. And the good things that happened always feel huge. The mind distorts in both directions, but imaginary suffering always takes up more space than it deserves. Just think for a minute all the worries that run everyday in your head, then think of how many of them actually happen. 

Anxiety is basically time travel. It's the gap between now and some imagined future, filled with problems that don't exist yet. And might never exist. Right here, right now, most of what's torturing you isn't real.

So next time you're suffering, ask yourself: is this actually happening? Or am I rehearsing? If it's rehearsal, stop. The rehearsal doesn't prepare you. Doesn't make you stronger. It just makes you suffer twice: once in your head, once for real. And sometimes only in your head, because the real thing never shows up. When the bad thing comes, if it comes, you'll handle it. You always have.
