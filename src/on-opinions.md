# On Opinions


Be curious, not judgmental. Not every situation needs your opinion. Not every controversy requires you to pick a side. There’s a power on understanding that you can just observe things. Most of what you have opinions about doesn't actually benefit from your opinion. And every opinion costs attention. 

The biggest obstacle to learning is thinking you already know. Because when you think you already understand something, you stop looking. You defend instead of discover. You judge instead of observe.

Mark Twain says it much better: “It ain't what you don't know that gets you into trouble. It's what you know for sure that just ain't so.”

Curiosity and judgment can't coexist. When you're genuinely curious about something, like why does this person think this way, what am I missing, you're not judging. You're investigating. Investigation is open, while judgment is closed.

Next time you feel the pull to judge, just ask: do I actually need an opinion here? Often the answer is no. And in that no is a strange kind of relief.
