# On Identity


A big identity feels solid. Anchored. Reassuring. But it's also a leveraged position. The more you invest in a single version of yourself, the more you must protect it from volatility. And life son, is a volatile place. 
When we’re too solid on who we are, we begin writing for reputation instead of curiosity, speaking to maintain coherence instead of truth. What starts as integrity ends as inertia. 
The irony is exquisite: the more we know who we are, the less room there is to become.

I've caught myself doing this. Building an idea of who I am and then spending more energy protecting that idea than actually living. Filtering opportunities through the question "is this me?" instead of "is this interesting?" Turning down things that didn't match the story, not because they were wrong, but because they were unfamiliar. Identity is useful until it becomes a cage. And the tricky part is that the cage feels like home. You don't notice you're in it because everything inside it is comfortable and everything outside it feels like a threat to who you are. But who you are is not a fixed thing. It's not a fact you discover once and defend forever. It's a direction you keep choosing. And sometimes choosing means letting go of a version of yourself that served you well but doesn't anymore. 
The eagle from the awareness story didn't just lack awareness, it had an identity problem. It knew exactly who it was, it was a chicken, and that was its prison. 
Keep your identity small, the less you need to be someone specific, the more you can become.
