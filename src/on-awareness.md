# On Awareness


The unaware life is not worth living. In my mind, either you're shaping your life, or your life is shaping you. What decides the direction is one thing only: whether you're awake, conscious, or not. Everything in this book comes back to that word. Awareness. A man finds an eagle's egg and puts it in a chicken coop. The eagle hatches, grows up with the chickens. Scratches the dirt, clucks, flies short and low the way chickens do. One day it looks up and sees an enormous bird crossing the sky without effort, riding the wind. "What's that?!!" the eagle asks. "That's an eagle," a chicken says. "King of the birds. But forget about it, you're a chicken." And the eagle lived and died a chicken. Because that's what it believed it was.

How many things do I do simply because I've always done them? How many beliefs about who I am have I never actually tested? The eagle didn't lack the ability to fly. It lacked the awareness that flying existed. If only it would test and ask and challenge. Paul Graham once used a powerful metaphor for the way people failed many years to use the internet's real potential: when an animal has been in a cage long enough, when you open the door, you sometimes have to poke it with a stick to get it to leave. Something similar happens when you live unaware of what's possible. And that's what awareness is. It's not knowing more. It's seeing what's already there. 


I try to ask myself constantly, what cage may I not be seeing and need to be poked of?
